The time-window was pretty short and there was only one extra developer on the project but we made it and I could transfer the project to perpetuate and stabilize the business while I moved to other opportunities. At the same company a few years before I was invited to fix a pressing issue that could have led to severe liabilities. An important pipeline was stalling and risked putting many business process to a halt. Institutional knowledge was partly lost and mostly transmitted by oral lore. In both cases these projects were recognized as tactical endeavours. That is, one-time feats to achieve, with a fair amount of freedom to step out of typical company guidelines and processes. Even though the scale and timeframe differed. What made both these critical topics click was not the technology, but the team dynamics. We made some internal presentations for these, but I also wanted to share a bit to the outside world how I approach these projects. This article discusses my recipe to tactical projects.

A checklist of communication tools

Successful projects require communication. If we stretch a bit, all the tools from the checklist I am about to give are about communication. Indeed, successful require communication. Communicating efficiently is hard because abundant communication is noisy, you cannot just fix communication issues with more communication but with better communication. In tactical projects, I believe that better means intentional communication.

In depth review of the checklist items

This section just dives deeper in each topic.

the jargon document

  • tribal knowledge is bad

  • oftentimes multiple concepts hide behind a same term because they overlap, same word != same meaning

  • humans have a tendency to be shy, for instance, they won‚Äôt dare asking what an acronym or a keyword means because they are new to the company

  • you do not want a glossary, you want a Rosetta stone

  • example spreadsheet

the periodic-meetings doc

  • have a template for a meeting for recurring topic du jour typically: ongoing topics/blockers/HRs

  • at end of meeting, prepare an entry for the next meeting with the template

  • often review the template

  • assign names to people

  • people should be encouraged to pre-fill the doc before meetings

  • keep growing the doc until it becomes unbearably slow (ctrl+F on a topic, on a date, on a person has likely a better UX and performance than anything else)

  • example periodic-meeting notes document

open-point/arbitrage

  • many decisions must be taken, software architecture, de-prioritizing some key bug

  • the leader must be able to judge, motivate her decision, and record by stressing-out the decision made

  • encourage people to submit new open-points, link to them

  • useful to have a table of content to quickly see what has been decided yet

  • example open-points document

one communication channel for people

  • have a chat with all the stakeholder
  • avoid multiplying chats because it encourages cross-talks
  • people are used to have dozens of channel but do not force them
  • mostly breaks Ctrl-F
  • exceptions are if you have regulatory requirements (e.g., some info must stay more confidential), but for this, private chats probably are a better venue as they feel more transient

one communication channel for robots

  • an key exception to the above rule are automation and robots (CI is breaking, alerts and notificaitons)
  • they pollute
  • a good way for your tech-team to decompress, share memes that would be borderline in the main communication channel
  • you can invite non-tech if they are power-user enough or if they know how to keep calm when they see scary words like ‚Äòalert messages‚Äô

one main-doc which links all the other docs

  • the checklist is rather narrow, you may need other docs (pre-existing specifications, slides)

  • bookmarks UX are really bad, hard to share, people are not familiar with the tools (unlike your shared doc tool)

  • each time a link or document pops, add an entry to the doc, the entry should have a catchy title (see jargon, the title will become the de-facto name for the doc among the team)

  • bang that rule on people‚Äôs head, nudge them towards making this doc their only bookmark

  • do not over organize until you have some acute need, again, ctrl+F will do wonders, especially if docs have catchy names

Behavioral aspects

The maestro analogy

  • people will have various instrument: some play brass whereas some others play strings
  • people will have various styles: some play jazz, some prefer classical, you may need them to play modern chamber music
  • as a lead you have one key role: set the tempo
  • you must ensure they tune-in, you do not know how they should play but you know how it should sound

Ceremonies

  • no fixed ceremony will solve your problems
  • the main benefit I find to ceremonies are their agenda-locking power: people have no excuse to miss them
  • typically you will need some frequent meeting to go through the periodic-meeting notes
  • daily may be too strict, weekly to slow
  • you set the tempo, so you should adapt depending on the pace, the urgency, and the delivery you expect (it happened that we made one meeting every other week, whereas sometimes as deadlines or key delivery approach we meet twice a day or assemble in the same place and pair)

Directing with boundaries

  • people sincerely hate micro-management, seriously
  • you won‚Äôt have the luxury to review everything and will need to cut corners, eroding your faith in the ‚Äútrust but verify‚Äù adage
  • I prefer ‚Äúset the boundary and trust‚Äù
  • you must learn how to direct with boundaries

    • motivate, and rephrase them, formally and informally (e.g., the zorgulino criterion is that all time you make a change, you need to see only diffs in the positive direction in the all-accounts total; this value is non-decreasing of version numbers etc.)
